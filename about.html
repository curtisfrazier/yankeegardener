About the gardener

Nice to meet you. I'm Maggie.

A born-and-raised New Yorker, a recent retiree, and a lifelong plant lover who is learning that everything she knew about gardening stops working somewhere around the Georgia line.

Forty years of Northern gardening

I grew up in Queens and spent most of my adult life in Brooklyn, which is not exactly farm country. But the gardening bug does not care where you live. My first garden was a row of coffee cans on a windowsill, then a fire escape crowded with geraniums and basil, and eventually a hard-won plot in a community garden where I learned everything the slow way, from neighbors who had been growing tomatoes in that same patch of city dirt for decades.

For most of my career I worked long hours as a public school administrator. Gardening was my pressure valve. I memorized our frost dates, started seeds under a shop light in the spare room, and planned the whole year around that glorious window between the last freeze in spring and the first one in fall. I cherished the rhythm of it. Bulbs in October, a long cold rest, then the first green shoots of crocus pushing through late snow. If you have ever gardened in the Northeast, you know that anticipation. You earn every single bloom.

Why we moved to Florida

When my husband Tom and I both retired within a year of each other, we did the math on another twenty winters of icy sidewalks and decided we were done. Our daughter had settled near Tampa, the cost of a small house felt within reach, and the idea of gardening twelve months a year sounded like a dream. We sold the apartment, gave away the snow boots, and drove south with a car full of houseplants and a head full of confidence.

That confidence lasted about three weeks. My first summer here, I planted a bed the way I always had, watered it like a Northern garden, and watched half of it cook, rot, or simply give up. The lettuce bolted before I could blink. The soil, if you can even call that pale sand soil, would not hold a drop of water. And the growing calendar I had trusted my whole life was suddenly upside down.

It turns out that moving to paradise does not make you a better gardener. It makes you a beginner again. And honestly, I have come to love that.

The culture shock of Zone 10

Up North I gardened in Zone 6 and 7. Down here I am squarely in Zone 10, and the difference is bigger than a couple of numbers on a seed packet. Our hard season is not winter, it is summer, when the heat and humidity become so punishing that many plants simply stop growing and wait it out. The cool, comfortable months from October through April are our prime growing window, which means I now plant tomatoes in the fall and harvest greens in January. Reverse everything you learned in a Northern garden and you are about halfway there.

Then there is the cast of characters. Lubber grasshoppers the size of my thumb. Love bugs that show up twice a year like clockwork. Iguanas that treat my hibiscus like a salad bar. Afternoon thunderstorms that drop two inches of rain and then vanish. It is a wilder, louder, more dramatic kind of gardening than the gentle seasons I grew up with, and after a rough start, I would not trade it.

What you can expect from this blog

Yankee Gardener is my honest field journal. I write for the transplanted Northerner staring at a yard full of sand and palmettos, wondering where to even begin. I write for the snowbird who wants something blooming when they arrive in November. And I write for anyone who, like me, loves plants enough to keep failing at them cheerfully until they get it right.

Here is what I try to deliver in every post:

  • Plants I have actually grown, in my own sandy beds, with the honest verdict on what thrived and what sulked.
  • Seasonal guidance that fits the Gulf Coast, not a generic calendar written for someone in Ohio.
  • Beginner-friendly explanations of the things that confused me most, from soil amendment to salt tolerance to that upside-down planting schedule.
  • A few stories, because gardening is supposed to be a joy, not a chore, and the iguana really is hilarious when he is not eating my flowers.

If any of that sounds like your kind of place, I am so glad you found me. Wander through the blog, and if you have a question or just want to swap notes from your own garden, please say hello. There is always room on the porch for one more gardener.
