From Tulips to Tropicals: What This Yankee Had to Unlearn About Gardening in Florida

When people hear that I retired from New York to Florida, they assume the gardening got easier. Sunshine all year, no more frozen ground, surely a green thumb just gets greener down here. I believed that too, right up until my first Florida summer turned a perfectly good vegetable bed into a soggy, sun-scorched disappointment. The truth is that moving from the Northeast to the subtropics is not a small adjustment. It is a top-to-bottom rewiring of nearly everything I thought I knew. Here is what I had to unlearn.

1. The seasons are flipped

Up North, summer is the main event. You wait all winter for it, you cram every tomato and zinnia into those few warm months, and then you mourn when frost ends the show. In Florida, summer is the season to survive, not celebrate. June through September brings such relentless heat, humidity, and downpour that many plants simply stop producing and hunker down. Our glorious growing window is the opposite end of the calendar, roughly October through May, when the air finally cools to something pleasant.

This took me an embarrassingly long time to accept. I kept trying to plant tomatoes in April, the way I always had, only to watch them set no fruit once the nights stayed warm. Now I start my tomatoes, peppers, and most vegetables in early fall and harvest right through the mild winter. Planting my cool-weather greens in January still feels slightly illegal, like I am getting away with something. I am not. It is just Florida.

2. There is no real winter rest

One of the rhythms I loved most up North was dormancy. The garden went to sleep, I pored over seed catalogs by the radiator, and everything got a long, restorative pause. Down here, that pause never really comes. The lawn keeps growing. Weeds keep sprouting. Many plants flower in January. It is wonderful and a little exhausting, because the garden never fully lets you off the hook.

The upside is enormous, though. I can grow something edible or blooming in every single month of the year. The trick was learning to pace myself instead of going full throttle the way Northern summers demand. A Florida garden is a marathon, not a sprint, and I had to stop gardening like the clock was about to run out.

I spent forty years racing the frost. Down here, nothing is chasing me. That was the hardest habit to break, and the most freeing.

3. The soil is sand, and it does not forgive

My Brooklyn community plot had rich, dark, worked-over soil that held moisture and nutrients like a sponge. My Florida yard, by contrast, is essentially pale sand. Water pours straight through it. Nutrients wash away almost as fast as I add them. The first time I watered a new bed, the moisture was gone in what felt like minutes, and my plants looked thirsty by afternoon.

The fix is constant organic matter. I add compost, composted manure, and leaf mulch again and again, because sandy soil burns through organic material quickly in this heat. I mulch heavily to slow evaporation. I water more often but watch carefully, since soggy roots are just as deadly as dry ones here. If you are coming from good Northern loam, make peace early with the fact that you are not amending your soil once. You are amending it forever.

4. Full sun means something different

A plant tag that says full sun was written for a gentler climate. Northern full sun and Florida full sun are not the same animal. Our sun is intense, the days are long, and afternoon rays in July can fry tender leaves that would happily bask all day in New England. Many things labeled full sun actually appreciate a little afternoon shade down here. I lost more than a few plants before I learned to read my yard's light hour by hour rather than trusting the tag.

5. The pests are bigger and bolder

I traded aphids and Japanese beetles for lubber grasshoppers the size of my thumb, love bugs that arrive in clouds twice a year, and the occasional iguana treating my hibiscus like a buffet. The cast of garden visitors here is louder and more dramatic. Some I hand-pick, some I tolerate, and some I have simply learned to plant around. It is a wilder kind of gardening, and after the initial shock, I have grown rather fond of the drama.

6. New plants, new love

The hardest goodbye was to my beloved spring bulbs. Tulips, daffodils, and lilacs need a winter chill that simply does not happen here. For a while I grieved them. Then I met the plants that replaced them, and oh, what a trade. Bougainvillea spilling over a fence in impossible magenta. Firebush humming with butterflies. Plumbago in the softest sky blue. Crotons glowing like stained glass. I did not lose a garden when I moved south. I gained a completely different one.

The mindset that finally worked

If I could hand my newly arrived self one piece of advice, it would be this: stop fighting Florida and start listening to it. Every time I tried to force my Northern playbook onto this place, I failed. The moment I treated myself as a beginner again, asked local gardeners what worked, and let the climate set the schedule, everything got easier and a lot more fun. The tulips are gone, but the tropicals are here, and they are spectacular.

If you have just made the same leap, I hope this saves you a season or two of frustration. Read on for the specific plants that have earned a permanent home in my sandy beds, and come tell me what is growing in yours.
